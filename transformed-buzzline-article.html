The Sanford Municipal Center was a hive of activity Monday morning as a new microtransit service was celebrated with a ribbon-cutting ceremony.

Buzzline is the first city-county microtransit partnership of its kind in North Carolina, offering affordable, app-based rides within a designated service zone that connects riders to work, school, medical appointments and more. The name Buzzline reflects Sanford's identity as a Bee City USA community.

First-of-Its-Kind City-County Microtransit Partnership Launches in Rural North Carolina

"It absolutely took a village to get where we are today," Brennon Fuqua, director of NCDOT's Integrated Mobility Division, told the crowd.

Lisa Mathis, vice chair of the NC Board of Transportation, Division 8, told the audience she enjoyed all of the "buzzing around."

"It's a pleasure to be in my hometown," she said. "It's truly a great day for Sanford and Lee County. This innovative ride sharing program fills an important need for people lacking reliable transportation."

Mathis said Buzzline is a new way to keep people connected.

"It's the first of its kind with the city/county cooperation," she said. "I can't wait to make a beeline to ride the Buzzline."

Lee County Commission Chair Kirk Smith thanked all of the entities involved in making the project come to fruition.

"Nothing compares to what we're doing," he said of the cooperation. "This will help citizens get around to jobs, medical appointments and more. Not everybody in our community has access to cars."

Sanford Mayor Rebecca Wyhof Salmon built on what the others had said, noting that it was "an important step forward."

"This is something we can all be proud of," she said. "We look forward to being the leading model across the state."

Salmon noted that the project was part of the "last mile" answer to people getting to work, school, medical appointments and other things.

"This is more than a ride," she said. "It is an opportunity. The program shows what is possible when we work together. It is a model of collaboration that reflects the best of who we are. Together, we're going to keep moving forward."

Service Details and Operational Scale

Buzzline services will begin with a soft launch on Wednesday, Nov. 12, operating weekdays from 6 a.m. to 6 p.m.

Full service will begin on Monday, Dec. 1, with expanded hours of operation as follows:

  • Monday to Friday: 6 a.m. to 8 p.m.
  • Saturday and Sunday: 7 a.m. to 7 p.m.

The fare is $5 per ride, or $10 for a round trip. The fare is per ride, not per person. Groups of up to five passengers can ride together without an increase in cost, according to officials.

Trips can be booked using the Buzzline app, online at buzzlinemicro.com or by phone at 919-776-7201.

Based on rider demand, the designated service area could be expanded.

This new service was developed from a feasibility study conducted in 2022 by the N.C. Department of Transportation and the Sanford/Lee County Planning Department, which found that Sanford would benefit from a subsidized on-demand transit program.

Buzzline is funded through a $3.4 million grant from NCDOT, which seeks to expand high quality, on-demand transit services in rural low-income communities through micro mobility services. The grant will offset services for the pilot program through December 2027.

The service will be operated by the County of Lee Transportation System (COLTS).

Buying Triggers

Sanford and Lee County's microtransit launch signals immediate technology procurement needs for rural communities exploring on-demand transit solutions. The $3.4 million NCDOT grant funding model creates a replicable template for other rural communities seeking state funding for similar programs.

  • Microtransit software platforms with mobile app booking capabilities and real-time vehicle tracking for zone-based service delivery
  • Demand-responsive scheduling systems to optimize multi-passenger routing within defined service zones
  • Automated vehicle location (AVL) systems for fleet management and performance monitoring throughout the pilot program
  • Performance analytics platforms to measure ridership, service area utilization, and cost-per-trip metrics required for grant reporting through December 2027
  • Multi-channel booking systems integrating mobile app, web portal, and phone dispatch (919-776-7201) for accessibility compliance
  • Service area expansion tools to evaluate and implement zone boundary adjustments based on rider demand data

Lookalike Prospects

The following rural communities and transit operators face identical last-mile connectivity challenges and represent prime prospects for microtransit deployment:

  • Wilson County Transit (Wilson, NC) - Similar rural service area population (~50,000), currently exploring on-demand solutions to complement fixed-route service, eligible for NCDOT rural transit grants
  • Alamance County Transportation Authority (Burlington, NC) - 165,000 population county with mixed urban-rural service needs, seeking city-county partnership models for microtransit expansion
  • First Transit (Gastonia Operations, NC) - 25+ vehicles serving Gastonia Transit System, contracted operator evaluating microtransit integration to extend fixed-route coverage in suburban zones
  • Iredell County Area Transportation System (Statesville, NC) - County-operated transit serving 180,000 population, facing identical last-mile connectivity gaps between rural communities and employment centers
  • Transdev (High Point Transit Contract, NC) - 40+ vehicles, contracted operator for High Point Transit System considering microtransit pilots to serve industrial park employment corridors
  • Rowan Transit System (Salisbury, NC) - Small city (35,000 population) with county-wide service responsibility, seeking state grant opportunities for on-demand transit to connect rural residents to medical facilities
  • MV Transportation (Concord/Kannapolis Operations, NC) - Paratransit contractor serving Cabarrus County with 30+ vehicles, exploring microtransit expansion to improve medical appointment access for aging population
  • Harnett Area Rural Transit System (Lillington, NC) - Adjacent county to Lee County with 135,000 population, watching Buzzline pilot results to inform own microtransit feasibility planning

Market Implications

The Sanford-Lee County partnership establishes a new procurement model for North Carolina rural communities seeking to implement microtransit services. The NCDOT's Integrated Mobility Division has signaled strong support for replicating this city-county collaboration framework statewide.

The $3.4 million grant through December 2027 creates a 30-month evaluation period that will generate performance data critical for other rural communities preparing grant applications. NCDOT officials' presence at the ribbon-cutting indicates the state's commitment to scaling this model across Division 8 and other rural regions.

The $5 per ride fare structure and group accommodation (up to 5 passengers per trip) sets pricing expectations for similar rural microtransit programs. This affordability model, enabled by state subsidies, will influence procurement decisions for communities evaluating vendor proposals.

The service area expansion clause based on rider demand creates a precedent for phased microtransit deployment, reducing initial capital requirements while maintaining growth flexibility. This approach is particularly relevant for rural communities with limited budgets seeking to demonstrate proof-of-concept before full-scale implementation.

With COLTS operating the service in-house rather than contracting to a private operator, the model demonstrates the feasibility of existing rural transit systems expanding service portfolios to include on-demand capabilities without outsourcing operations.
